\begin{myConceptSteps}{To find the $x$- and $y$-intercepts of a line from its equation\dots}
    \myStep{$y\mapsto 0$}{
        Substitute $y=0$ in the original equation.
        This will give you an equation that only has $x$.
    }
    \myStep{solve}{Solve that equation for $x$. The solution is the $x$-intercept.}
    \myStep{$x\mapsto 0$}{
        Substitute $x=0$ in the original equation.
        This will give you an equation that only has $y$.
    }
    \myStep{solve}{Solve that equation for $y$. The solution is the $y$-intercept.}
\end{myConceptSteps}


It turns out that this method of finding the intercepts {\bfseries\itshape works for any equation},
but we're only worried about linear equations for now.
