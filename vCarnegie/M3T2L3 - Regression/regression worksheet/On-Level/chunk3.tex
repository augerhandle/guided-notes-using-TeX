\section*{Problem 3 --- COVID Hospitalizations}\label{sec:prob-3}
This table shows 
the number of people hospitalized in the US due to COVID for each month since June 2023.
% https://covid.cdc.gov/covid-data-tracker/#trends_weeklyhospitaladmissions_select_00
\begin{center}
    % \small
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
    \begin{tabular}{r|c|c}
        \toprule
            {\itshape date}
            & 
            \begin{tabular}{c}
                $x$\\
                {\tiny\itshape months since June 2023} \\
            \end{tabular}
            & 
            \begin{tabular}{c}
                $y$\\
                {\tiny\itshape \# hospitalizations (in thousands)}
            \end{tabular}
            \\
        \midrule 
        January 2024   & \gap{7} & 35.801\\
        December 2023  & \gap{6} & 22.885\\
        November 2023  & \gap{5} & 15.082\\
        October 2023   & \gap{4} & 17.156\\
        September 2023 & \gap{3} & 19.144\\
        August 2023    & \gap{2} & 10.521\\
        July 2023      & \gap{1} &  6.49 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}

Use \myDesmos to do \myEmph{linear}, \myEmph{quadratic}, and \myEmph{cubic} regressions on these data.
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Fill in the second column ({\itshape months since June 2023}).
        {\itshape 
            Be careful. The starting date is \fbox{June} 2023, but there are no data for that month.
        }
    \item Enter the $x$ and $y$ data into a \myDesmos $\mathtt{x_1}$--$\mathtt{y_1}$ table.
    \item Do a \myEmph{linear}, \myEmph{quadratic}, and \myEmph{cubic} regressions in \myDesmos{}.
    \item Write the regression equations, and find the values of $r^2$.
\end{itemize}
\begin{center}
    \footnotesize
    \setlength{\tabcolsep}{1.3em}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
    \begin{tabular}{l|l}
        \toprule
        {\itshape regression equation} & $r^2$ \\
        \midrule 
        $y=$ \gap{3.8785}$x$ + \gap{2.64} & $r^2 =$ \gap{0.7807}\\ 
        $y=$ \gap{0.47801}$x^2$ + \gap{0.05444}$x$ + \gap{8.3761} & $r^2 =$ \gap{0.8163}\\
        $y=$ \gap{0.58358}$x^3$ + \gap{-6.525}$x^2$ + \gap{23.981}$x$ + \gap{-12.633} & $r^2 =$ \gap{0.9526} \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}


% \vspace{\onelineskip}
\noindent
If $r^2$ is close to 1.0, the model is a ``good fit'' for the data.
Which model is a better fit, linear, quadratic, or cubic regression? 
Explain your reasoning.\\[0.65\onelineskip]
