\section{Negative exponents}

Suppose \gap{$b>1$}.
Then $f(x) = b^x$ is exponential \gap{growth}.
What about $g(x) = \left(\frac{1}{b}\right)^x$?
If $b>1$, 
then \gap{$\frac{1}{b}<1$}.
So $g(x)$ is exponential \gap{decay}.
We can rewrite $g$ as $g(x) = b^{-x}$. 

\begin{tcolorbox}[center,colback=white,width=6.5in,]
    So
    if $b>1$ and the \gap{coefficient} on $x$ in the exponent is \gap{negative},
    \[ g(x) = b^{-rx} \quad\text{\itshape (the coefficient is $-r$),} \]
    then $g$ is 
    \gap{exponential decay}.
\end{tcolorbox}


In general,
\begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
    \Large
    \begin{tabular}{c||c|c}
        {} & {\bfseries\itshape growth} & {\bfseries\itshape  decay} \\
        \hline
        $y=b^x$ &  $b>1$ & $0<b<1$ \\
        $y=b^{rx}$
            & 
            $b>1$, \quad $r>0$
            &
            $b>1$, \quad $r<0$
            \\
    \end{tabular}
\end{center}
